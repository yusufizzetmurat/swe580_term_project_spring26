# January 29, 2026

[[Industry_Talk_Jan29]] with Dr. Sharma from Google DeepMind was genuinely excellent. The point about prompt sensitivity in production — that a model update can silently break a prompt that worked before — is something I hadn't thought about carefully. It immediately made the [[Prompt_Optimizer]] project feel more important, not just as an experiment but as a necessary engineering practice.

The "hallucination rate on grounded queries" metric she mentioned is going into the [[Evaluation_Framework]]. Checking whether the generated answer is supported by the retrieved text is exactly the kind of faithfulness metric we're missing.

Finished the [[Optimization_Methods]] notes in the evening. Writing about learning rate warmup for transformers helped me understand why our training without warmup was unstable in the first two weeks — we were taking large gradient steps into a bad region before the model had any sense of the loss landscape.

[[Text_Summarizer]] experiments are running. T5 results look better than BART so far on the first validation checkpoint, but it's too early to be sure.

## Reflection

Dr. Sharma said something that stuck with me: "Researchers optimize for average performance. Engineers optimize for worst-case behavior." Papers maximize metrics; products minimize failures. Most of what I'm building sits somewhere between the two, and I should think more about the failure modes.
